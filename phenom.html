What is a Black Hole?

Black holes are extreme cosmic objects with gravitational fields so powerful that nothing, not even light, can escape them. They are born from the remnants of massive stars that have exhausted their nuclear fuel. When such a star collapses under its own gravity, it can form a singularity—a point of infinite density—surrounded by an event horizon.

How is a Black Hole Created?

The life cycle of massive stars, at least 20 times the mass of our sun, involves a series of nuclear fusion reactions generating outward pressure that counteracts gravity. Once these stars exhaust their nuclear fuel, the diminished outward pressure leads to gravitational collapse. This collapse forms a singularity, an infinitesimally small point where gravity becomes infinitely strong. The outer boundary of a black hole is the event horizon, and anything crossing it, including light, is irresistibly drawn toward the singularity. Black holes come in various types, including Stellar Black Holes (formed from collapsing massive stars), Intermediate Black Holes (believed to result from merging stellar black holes), and Supermassive Black Holes (found at galaxy centers, influencing galactic structure and evolution).
